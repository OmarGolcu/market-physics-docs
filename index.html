Market Physics

A Python library of physics-inspired quantitative features for financial time series. Each of its 110 indicators borrows a measurement concept from a branch of physics — mechanics, gravitation, electromagnetism, thermodynamics, information theory, chaos, fractal geometry, random matrix theory — and applies it as a descriptive estimator over OHLCV market data.

Scope

Every output is a descriptive feature, not a trading signal. The library is not a buy/sell generator, not a strategy framework, and not a backtester. Physics names are analogies for the estimators — the library makes no claim that markets obey physical law, only that physical measurement concepts produce useful descriptive coordinates for market state.

Why econophysics features

Standard technical features describe price with price's own vocabulary: averages of it, ranges of it, ratios of it. Physics offers an alternative: a century of instruments built to measure any dynamical system — how disordered it is (entropy), how much energy a motion carries (mechanics), how strongly components couple (networks), whether fluctuations are self-similar (fractals), how far from equilibrium the system sits (thermodynamics). Applied to a return series, these instruments produce features that are often weakly correlated with classical technical indicators, which is precisely what makes them useful as inputs to research pipelines: they measure different axes of market state.

Each family chapter below opens with the physics — the original phenomenon, the governing relations, and how the estimator transplants them to market data — then documents every indicator: signature, physical picture, parameters, return contract, a runnable example, and the indicator computed on a shared reference dataset.

Conventions & errors

  • Warm-up — a rolling indicator with window w is NaN until enough observations exist; output stays aligned to the input index.
  • Returns — functions with a return_method parameter accept "pct" (percentage change, default) or "log" (log returns).
  • Windows are bars — every window, lookback, and period is measured in bars/candles of whatever timeframe you feed in, never clock time.
  • Validation — missing columns raise MissingColumnError; too few rows raise InsufficientDataError (both subclass MarketPhysicsError from market_physics.exceptions); out-of-range parameters raise ValueError.
  • Histogram bins — distribution-based indicators accept bins as "fd" (Freedman–Diaconis, default), "sqrt", "sturges", or an integer ≥ 2.

The reference dataset

Every chart in this documentation shows the indicator computed on the same synthetic OHLCV series: 800 bars through four engineered regimes — a low-volatility uptrend (bars 0–250), sideways chop (250–450), a high-volatility crash with a volume surge (450–560, shaded red), and a decaying-volatility recovery (560–800). Synthetic data is used deliberately: because the regimes are known by construction, you can verify that each indicator responds the way its physics says it should.

dynamics

Newtonian kinematics — velocity, force, work · 10 indicators

Classical mechanics begins with a single mapping: a body has a position x(t), and everything else — velocity, acceleration, momentum, force — is derived from how that position changes. The dynamics family makes the same move for markets: price is position. Once x(t) = P(t), the whole kinematic chain follows by differentiation:

v(t) = dx/dt  →  return of the close price
a(t) = dv/dt  →  change in the return
p(t) = m·v(t)  →  volume-weighted return (volume plays mass)
F(t) = m·a(t)  →  volume-weighted change in return

The interesting physics is in the role of mass. In mechanics, mass is the body's resistance to acceleration; here volume is the mass of a move. A 2% move on heavy volume carries more momentum — in the literal p = mv sense — than the same move on thin volume, and by Newton's second law it takes a proportionally larger force to reverse it. This is why the family distinguishes velocity (how fast price moves) from momentum and force (how much conviction is behind the motion).

Beyond the kinematic chain, the family borrows from oscillator theory and energetics. A damped oscillator loses amplitude at a rate set by its damping ratio; a trend that keeps shrinking in amplitude behaves the same way. Friction is motion lost to resistance; inertia is the tendency of a trend to persist; work (W = F·d) is what volume expends pushing price over a distance; and potential energy accumulates when price is displaced from its equilibrium — a compressed spring waiting to release.

When to reach for this family: trend diagnosis. It answers, in a common mechanical vocabulary, how fast the market is moving, whether the motion is speeding up or decaying, and how much mass is behind it.

Damping Ratio borrows the language of damped oscillators, where damping describes how quickly oscillation amplitude decays relative to a reference. Mapping price to position, it compares the short-horizon amplitude of returns against the long-horizon amplitude:

* short_amp -> rolling standard deviation of returns over short_window, * long_amp -> rolling standard deviation of returns over window, * ratio -> short_amp / long_amp.

Values below 1 describe short-term amplitude that is small relative to the longer horizon (recent oscillation is damped); values above 1 describe short-term amplitude that exceeds the longer horizon (recent oscillation is amplified). This is a descriptive amplitude ratio only — it is not a trading signal and carries no directional recommendation.

Impulse Energy borrows the mechanics idea of an impulse — a large force delivered over a short interval that produces a sudden change in motion. Mapping price to position, it measures how sharp a move is relative to its own recent volatility and scales that shock by the participating mass:

* shock -> abs(returns) / rolling_std(returns) (move size in units of its own recent volatility), * mass -> normalised volume (current volume relative to its rolling mean), * impulse -> shock * mass * scale.

Impulse Energy is **unsigned**: a sharp up-move and an equally sharp down-move of the same standardised magnitude and volume produce the same impulse. Higher values describe sudden, volume-backed shocks; values near zero describe calm, low-participation movement. This is a descriptive feature only — it is not a trading signal and carries no directional recommendation.

market_friction

from market_physics.dynamics import market_friction

    df: pd.DataFrame,
    window: int = 50,

Market Friction borrows the idea of a path that dissipates energy through resistance. Mapping price to position, it compares the total distance travelled by the return path against the net displacement over the same window:

* path_length -> sum of absolute returns (total ground covered), * net_displacement -> absolute sum of returns (straight-line progress), * friction -> 1 - net_displacement / path_length.

The value lives in [0, 1]. Near 0 describes efficient, directional movement (the path is nearly a straight line and almost all motion translates into net displacement). Near 1 describes noisy movement with little net displacement (the path wanders and cancels out, like motion lost to friction).

This is a descriptive feature only — it is not a trading signal and carries no directional recommendation.

market_potential_energy

from market_physics.dynamics import market_potential_energy

    df: pd.DataFrame,
    window: int = 100,
    *,
    equilibrium: Literal['vwap', 'sma'] = 'vwap',

Market Potential Energy borrows the idea of the potential energy stored in a displacement from equilibrium (as in a spring, U = 0.5 * k * x^2). Mapping price to position, the equilibrium is a rolling centre of price and the displacement is how far the current close sits from that centre:

* center -> a rolling equilibrium price ("sma" rolling mean or "vwap" volume-weighted average price), * displacement -> close - center, * potential -> displacement ** 2 (optionally divided by center ** 2 when normalize).

Market Potential Energy is **unsigned**: it grows with the squared distance from equilibrium regardless of which side of the centre price sits on. It describes how stretched price is relative to its rolling centre; it is explicitly **not** a mean-reversion signal and implies no expectation that price will return to the centre. This is a descriptive feature only — it is not a trading signal and carries no directional recommendation.

Trend Inertia borrows the classical-mechanics idea of inertia — a body's tendency to keep moving in the same direction. Mapping price to position, the return direction plays the role of the direction of motion; inertia measures how consistently that direction persists over the window.

When volume_weighted is False the value lives roughly in [0, 1]: high inertia describes a persistent directional regime (the return direction rarely flips), low inertia describes choppy, unstable direction (the sign flips often and cancels out). When volume_weighted is True, each direction is scaled by normalised volume so that high-participation bars contribute more to the persistence measure. Normalised volume acts as a mass/amplifier, so weighted values may exceed 1.0 when persistent direction coincides with above-average volume; weighted values are intentionally left unclipped so the amplification is preserved.

This is a descriptive feature only — it is not a trading signal and carries no directional recommendation.

gravity

Gravitation — mass, potential wells, orbits · 7 indicators

Newtonian gravity says mass curves the space around it: a body of mass M creates a potential U(r) = −GM/r, and objects roll downhill toward it. The gravity family transplants this to the price axis. Traded volume at a price level is mass: a level where a large amount of volume changed hands is a massive body sitting on the price line, and it attracts price the way a planet attracts a satellite.

potential at price P  =  Σi mi / (|P − Li| + ε)    — mi volume mass at level Li

The physical intuition is behavioural: at a heavily-traded level, many positions were opened. Those positions are memory — resting orders, break-even stops, anchored expectations — and they pull price back when it wanders. The family builds this in two steps. First, level detection: histogram the traded volume across price bins over a lookback, and pick out the local maxima of the mass distribution (gravity_levels, gravity_wells — the wells being the potential minima price tends to settle into). Second, field measurement: given the mass distribution, compute the potential at the current price, its gradient (the net pull, signed toward the dominant mass), the balance between masses above and below, and how far price has strayed from the nearest well — its orbital deviation.

Readers who know market-profile analysis will recognise high-volume nodes; the gravity framing adds the field formalism — a scalar potential, a gradient, an equilibrium — so the distance to a level and the strength of its pull combine into one number.

When to reach for this family: support/resistance in continuous form. Instead of binary "level broken / level held", you get a smooth potential landscape and price's position within it.

Electromagnetism — charge, current, induction · 9 indicators

Electromagnetism is the physics of charge in motion. A static charge creates an electric field (Coulomb); moving charge is a current that creates a magnetic field (Ampère); and a changing magnetic flux induces a voltage that opposes the change that made it (Faraday–Lenz). The fields family maps order-flow imbalance onto this structure: signed buying/selling pressure is charge.

q(t)  =  sign of the move × volume   — market charge
I(t)  =  dq/dt   — market current: the flow of charge
u ∝ E² + B²   — field energy density → electromagnetic pressure

From that one identification the rest of the vocabulary follows. Charge density is imbalance concentrated per unit time — sustained one-sided pressure rather than a single burst. Polarity is the sign structure of recent charge: a market flipping between positive and negative charge is depolarised; one accumulating same-signed charge is strongly polarised. Current is charge in motion — directional volume flow — and like a physical current it does work and dissipates. Induction is the Faraday move: when the flow changes rapidly, the market induces a response that resists the change (liquidity stepping in against an aggressive burst). Electromagnetic pressure sums the squared field strengths into an energy density — how much stress the combined fields put on the current price.

Where the dynamics family asks how price moves, the fields family asks how the order flow behind it is charged — its sign, concentration, flow rate, and the stresses those create.

When to reach for this family: pressure and imbalance diagnosis — detecting sustained one-sided flow, polarity flips, and aggressive bursts that the market is likely to resist.
